*This closes Section 1.5 Exercises.*

**Priority gradient for a quant-prep pass:**

- **Highest value:**
  - **19** (difference quotient for $5^x$) — derives the identity $\frac{5^{x+h} - 5^x}{h} = 5^x \cdot \frac{5^h - 1}{h}$. This is the exact form that leads into $\frac{d}{dx}5^x = 5^x \ln 5$ in Chapter 3. Do it.
  - **20** (the penny-doubling problem) — beautiful illustration of exponential vs. linear. Plan II pays $2^{30} - 1$ cents = \$10{,}737{,}418.23, ten times Plan I. Worth computing.
  - **23** (when does $e^x$ surpass $x^{10}$) — drives the "exponentials dominate polynomials" fact I flagged in Unit 1. The crossover is around $x \approx 35$, which is surprisingly late for such a steep polynomial.
  - **27, 28** (exponential regression on population data) — practical fitting pipeline. The technique generalizes to yield-curve fitting, decay-of-alpha modeling, and volatility term structure.
  - **30** (logistic family $\frac{1}{1 + a e^{bx}}$) — this is the **logistic function**, which shows up as (i) the activation function in logistic regression, (ii) the CDF of the logistic distribution, (iii) the model of constrained exponential growth (bounded populations, epidemic curves, technology adoption). Parameter sensitivity analysis here is directly relevant to ML/stats.

- **Medium value:**
  - **3–6** (common-screen comparisons) — visual pattern recognition.
  - **7–12** (graph transformations of exponentials) — mechanical practice.
  - **17, 18** (fit an exponential from two points) — the 2-parameter fit is the building block of every more-complex exponential regression.
  - **25, 26** (bacteria doubling) — direct application of $p(t) = p_0 \cdot 2^{t/T}$.

- **Skippable for you:**
  - **1, 2** (concept questions with no computation).
  - **13, 14** (write the equation after a shift) — trivial given Unit 1.
  - **15, 16** (domain-finding) — routine.

**Exercise 19 specifically deserves attention** because it's the exact calculation you'll redo in Chapter 3 to derive the derivative of $a^x$. Getting comfortable with $\frac{a^{x+h} - a^x}{h} = a^x \cdot \frac{a^h - 1}{h}$ now will make the $\frac{d}{dx}a^x$ derivation feel inevitable rather than clever.

**Exercise 30 specifically deserves attention** because the logistic function $\frac{1}{1 + a e^{bx}}$ (or its equivalent forms $\frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$, $\frac{e^x}{1 + e^x}$) is one of the five most-used functions in machine learning and probabilistic modeling. You'll meet it as the sigmoid activation, as the logit link in GLMs, as the CDF of the logistic distribution, and as the solution to constrained growth ODEs. Stewart introduces it casually here; treat it as a major landmark.
