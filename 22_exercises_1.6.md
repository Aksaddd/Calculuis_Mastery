## Real-world inverses (Exercises 19–20)

**19.** `[HINT]` The formula $C = \tfrac{5}{9}(F - 32)$, where $F \geq -459.67$, expresses the Celsius temperature $C$ as a function of the Fahrenheit temperature $F$. Find a formula for the inverse function and interpret it. What is the domain of the inverse function?

**20.** In the theory of relativity, the mass of a particle with speed $v$ is
$$m = f(v) = \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$$

*This closes Section 1.6 Exercises.*

**Priority gradient for a quant-prep pass:**

- **Highest value:**
  - **21–26** (formula inverse) — mechanical drills; 6 problems cements the 3-step recipe.
  - **53–54** (domain of $f$ and $f^{-1}$) — force you to track domain restrictions through composition of $\sqrt{}$, $\ln$, etc. This same tracking matters for inverse CDF / quantile computation.
  - **65, 66, 67, 68** (simplify $\text{trig}(\text{arctrig})$) — four drills on the right-triangle technique from Example 13. Essential for Chapter 7 trig substitution.
  - **73** (inverse of $f(x+c)$ and $f(cx)$) — build the intuition that shifts and scales interact predictably with inversion. Same pattern as coordinate changes.
- **High value (targeted):**
  - **19** (Celsius/Fahrenheit) — a concrete linear inverse; done in seconds.
  - **20** (relativistic mass inverse) — forces you to invert an algebraic expression to recover $v$ from $m$. Standard pattern.
  - **49(b)** ($\ln x + \ln(x-1) = 1$) — combine-logs-first trick, then exponentiate. Classic.
  - **57, 58** (bacteria + camera flash) — inverse of a growth/relaxation model. Concrete template for half-life, RC time constants, etc.
  - **43** (height of $\log_2 x$ reaches 3 ft) — visceral demonstration of log slowness. The answer comes out to absurdly far.
- **Medium value (skim or pick):**
  - **33–36** (exact log values) — basic identities; pick 4.
  - **37–39** (combine into single log) — 3 applications of Laws 1–3.
  - **47–52** (solve equations/inequalities) — standard algebraic manipulation; pick 4.
  - **59–64** (exact inverse trig values) — memorize unit circle.
- **Skippable:**
  - **1, 2, 31, 32** (pure concept Q's) — no computation.
  - **3–14** (is it 1-1?) — routine; pick 3.
  - **40, 41, 42** (change of base for specific numbers) — trivial with a calculator.

**Specific high-value items worth focus:**

- **Exercise 65** ($\cos(\sin^{-1} x) = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$) is *the* companion identity to Example 13. Together they're the two you'll use for trig substitution in Chapter 7: $\cos(\arcsin x)$ and $\cos(\arctan x)$. Prove both.
- **Exercise 68** ($\cos(2\arctan x)$) requires the double-angle identity $\cos(2\theta) = \frac{1 - \tan^2 \theta}{1 + \tan^2 \theta}$ combined with $\tan(\arctan x) = x$. A good Chapter-7-prep drill.
- **Exercises 55–56 (CAS)** — if you want to run them concretely, Mathematica, Wolfram Alpha, or sympy will handle them. Not essential if you skip.
- **Exercise 73 specifically** — the algebraic rule is $(f(x + c))^{-1} = f^{-1}(x) - c$ and $(f(cx))^{-1} = f^{-1}(x)/c$. Useful to have internalized for change-of-variables arguments throughout calc.
